Diabetes Readmittance

Motivation

Hospital readmissions are incredibly damaging on multiple fronts: healthcare cost, patient health, and time spent. By diagnosing which factors contribute to the hospital readmission rate, we will be able to tackle the problem head-on to bring about a huge boon to an already congested healthcare system in the United States. We especially focus on diabetes because there is relatively little research focused on hospitalized diabetes patients even though the burden of the cost of constant returns are rising significantly. By predicting whether or not a patient will be readmitted in the future, medical professionals can be alerted to prevent future readmissions by changing their treatment or expectations with a patient.

Data Acquisition

The data was acquired by using the UCI machine learning repository database. The dataset is described to be 10 years of clinical care at 130 US hospitals with 50 features representing patient and hospital outcomes. For further detail, check the reference below.

2. Cleaning & Encoding the Variables

The age variable only has text such as '[0-10)]' as categories for age ranges. Although this is still useful in that it is a category, we know that it should be a numerical value that has a relationship with each of the different ranges. Those aged 90-100 are obviously much older and more likely to be at risk of illnesses than those who are young adults such as the 20-30 range. Therefore, we rehash these values into their median values.

Next, we encode binary categorical values such as "Male" and "Female" in the gender column to be simply 0 and 1. We do the same for the change and diabetesMed columns as well. Similarly, we do this for the medicine columns as well. The information they provide with 4 different categories - 'No', 'Steady', 'Up', and 'Down' are simplified to 'Drug given' vs. 'Drug not given' to simplify the data frame without losing any significant information. When we group the distributions by the target variable to look at the ratio between the binary category, we see the distributions are more normal - this way the algorithm does not focus on the extreme errors as much.

3. Simplifying Hospital Codes and ICD-9

Having more than 900 categories for three features translates to an absolute nightmare in both an algorithmic computational standpoint as well as human understanding of our data. By using the information provided in the

the arcane coding system can be truncated down into 17 categories. For example, codes that begin with 0 and up to 139 are infectious and parasitic diseases that can be encoded into a category of 'A', while those up to 239 are neoplasms in category 'B', and so on.

Similarly, the admission type, admission source, and discharge disposition identifiers have a distinct mapping for multiple situations that can be further simplified. With all three columns, the categories would have transformed into something similar to having all outpatient discharges into one category, while the hash that included dead patients were dropped.

Finally, we create a dictionary of all of the features mapped to its corresponding data type. Those that are categorical were transformed into dummy variables to change qualitative facts into numeric substitutes (0 and 1) to create machine-readable, preprocessed data to be analyzed.

Hypothesis Testing & Feature Selection

At this point, we have 93 features that contribute to the target variable. Having too many features will increase model fitting time, clutter the model with noise, and perhaps more importantly will lose out on human comprehension and focus. Remember, our goal is to prioritize on specific features so that we understand how humans may possibly intervene in the treatment of a patient, and having more features may detract from that goal.

As our target variable is a binary categorical variable, we use a chi-squared test to calculate the p-value of our categorical features and then calculate the value of Cramer's V to understand its correlation. For our numerical features we will use a t-test to obtain the p-value and a point-biserial correlation function to obtain the correlation.

(Very) Naïve Models

Now, there needs to be a baseline accuracy to compare our models against to have a good idea of how it is performing. Zeroes, Ones, and Random values were used to get the performance of the most naïve models. A simple explanation for these were to have all of the target values be either all zeroes, all ones, or a random assortment of zeroes and ones. The zeroes model and ones model gets an accuracy score equal about the same as our ratio of data. Our random predictions results in a mean value of our zeroes and ones prediction - exactly 50%.

Model Selection

Our data has around ~95,000 samples and this is a labeled classification problem. The problem we are trying to solve is to provide a tool which can detect whether a patient will be readmitted in the future based on their medical history. This kind of tool would be used ideally to provide medical staff a list of patients that should be a targeted population for scrutinized care. In other words, a warning flag to preemptively change their workflow approach in a patient's treatment. As such, we are looking for a model that is high enough in accuracy, but can quickly iterate through the process of training on a hospital's dataset and spitting a likely prediction out.

In choosing our models we consider a couple of different approaches. For a classification problem with less than 100k samples we can use a KNN, however our data is quite noisy so it may not have the high accuracy we would like. A decision tree will be useful as it can handle some missing values, is fast in iterations, and simple to use. Due to our large number of categorical variables with many categories, the decision tree may give a biased response. From our heatmap, we can see that most the variables are weakly or very weakly correlated with each other. This allows us to explore a naïve Bayes solution, that may perform well with this dataset that is not incredibly large. Finally, we can use logistic regression as a simple model that is easy to implement with low computational power. It will be less prone to overfitting as we have preprocessed the data in such a way to lower the dimensionality, and could be extended to multi-class classification if we chose to further our model development in that way. As a benchmark, Linear SVM is used. The SVM model should result poorly because the dataset is not linearly separable as it uses real world data and many, if not most, patients share similar features. In other words, there will be a lot of noise.

Ensemble methods usually have a higher performance than single, simple models as well as overly complex neural network models on medium sized, tabular data. And, we fully expect them to do so here as well. Random Forest, a parallel ensemble method that decreases variance via bagging, will average multiple estimates from trees on different subsets of data. On the other hand, boosting will sequentially generate the base learning models to change weights on different features after evaluating its loss function and therefore decrease bias. XGBoost will use (extreme) gradient boosting which minimizes the loss function via a gradient descent algorithm.

Parameter Tuning

KNN

With KNN, elbow method is used to find the lowest k value that is nearest the lowest error value. Choosing the lowest k value allows us to keep the model robust by keeping it free of noise but also has enough predictive power- So we choose k = 10.

Random Forest

Hyper-parameter tuning using gridsearch can quickly iterate through the parameters in a random forest model. We can visually confirm the trend from the results before committing to a specific parameter. As an example, we can look at max_depth below:

The point at which the Train AUC and Test AUC diverges drastically is usually the indicator for the lowest error from the bias-variance tradeoff. We can see this happen around the 8-10 range for max_depth. Even within this range, the ensemble model needs to be as simple as possible to keep the model from overfitting and increasing variance on the data. Therefore our choice is 8. A similar process is done for multiple parameters for the random forest model to properly tune these dials before training the model.

Evaluation

The results of the evaluation of the models can be seen below. As expected, the Random Forest and XGBoost were able to do the best on the cross validation score and the SVM model performed the worst. The data must have been more noisy than previously thought, as KNN performed almost as poorly as the SVM. However, we see that most of the models were well within a 10% range of each other and did not greatly outperform our naïve models. In our model, what we would like to see is a high recall value for our medical setting because we do not want to miss false negative cases of patients that may be sick. On the other hand, being more cautious with patients that may be a false positive does not have as dire consequences. Naïve Bayes has the highest recall out of all the models while the linear SVM recall is extremely low.

Here, we can see the "best" model depends on which criteria we abide by. If we wanted to do our best to not miss and false negative cases we may opt for the Naïve Bayes model while sacrificing some of the precision of the ensemble models and the speed that random forest provides. At the same time, Logistic Regression and Random Forest, while higher in their cross validation score and speed, they miss out on the recall capabilities. And finally, XGBoost does the best in its cross validation score with moderate precision and recall values amongst the top models, but greatly sacrifices speed. This array of possibilities is a great demonstration of the "No Free Lunch" Theorem, where every model has its different tradeoffs and it is determinate upon different factors (business value, practical usage, regulatory obligations) that guide our choice.

As XGBoost has been chosen as the highest accuracy model, we can extract the feature importance and see that by far the number of inpatient visits is the most important. This may be an obvious answer to us because those who are most likely to return to a hospital are those who have already visited the hospital many times. However, it's interesting that it is heavily favored in comparison to all other features. One may conclude that receiving wrong diagnoses - hence the increasing number of diagnoses may be just as important to readmittance, or being in critical condition such as the number of emergency room visits. Furthermore, what's surprising is how low on the list the glucose test results is, considering one's test results may dictate whether or not a patient returns to the hospital in the future.

Conclusion

Winner - XGBoost Model

Within the workflow of a hospital, real-time data that pours into a medical record system would not exceed the level at which XGBoost can perform in a practical manner. Although the XGBoost model may take the longest amongst these different models, it only takes about a minute maximum to train and predict. It would be instead, far more realistic to pursue the greatest accuracy model that is perhaps trained with new data once a day. Therefore, my choice is the XGBoost model. My recommendation for a hospital is to have a system that monitors (at least) the top 5 most important features of patients. Changing the workflow to double check and give extra care to these patients so that they do not have to be readmitted could prevent future work and patient health being compromised.

In review, the model did not do incredibly well compared to the naïve models. Some of the shortcomings may be in using noisy data with some missing values that were not left out. Some data may have been messy and incorrectly recorded as well. My choices in having many of the categorical variables that were not as important be a factor in the model may have introduced more noise rather than information gain. And instead brought in the curse of dimensionality where it could have been easily avoided. Certainly, the model would need to be reiterated to build a more accurate model, that focuses a bit more on increasing the recall.

For future work, I could explore PCA and see how the data could have been reduced in dimensionality and preprocessed differently. This could provide a more robust, mathematical analysis rather than using domain knowledge and intuition as a guide. Another possibility is to use a log standardization for the numerical variables to make the variables more comparable by mapping the data to a more gaussian distribution.
